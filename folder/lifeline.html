Equality and Love in Latter-day Saint Doctrine

The article explores the deep relationship that Latter-day Saint doctrine has between equality and love. It says that in order to raise a child to a place of equality, someone above them must make sacrifices for an equal in order show true and pure love. The thought is realized in the Atonement of Jesus, where people are made equal heirs with Him by reason of His greater sacrifice (Doctrine and Covenants [D&C] 88:107). "Parental love in mortality emulates godly love. Those with healthy parental love make sacrifices so that their children may one day stand as their equals, and be not only their children, but also their friends" (Hudson & Miller, ch4, p. 40). Therefore, parental love is shown as a guidelines where sacrifices are made so that children can finally stand as equals and loved ones, reflecting the love of God. A Zion community lives the fullness of this commandment. Zion, as we know, is a place where the Saints are equal in both heavenly and temporal things (D&C 70:14; 78:5-7). Therefore, In order to create Zion communities where equality rules in both the heavenly and human realms, "we must love thy neighbors and remove any discordance between the ideal and the lived situation" (Hudson & Miller, ch4, p. 40).

Stewards in Equal Partnership

Family stewardships should be understood in terms of their responsibilities obligations to one's spouse, not power over one's spouse. Moreover, I will listed down some key facts about Stewardship in family partnership. Stewardship in equal partnership is about roles and responsability that couples need to do in their family. For example, collaborating with one another based on "managing the family duties, making right decision, and support each others personal goals" for improvement of their family and marriage. In order to have a firm and happy family, they need to express one another emotions and feelings about the roles that I just mentioned. And they must able to come up with an equal mindset on each roles.

    Shared Decision-Making and Responsibilities:

    In a fair partnership in couples, stewardship is defined by mutual engagement with family management and decision-making. This approach stands in contrast with established examples, which give one partner—typically the husband—a significant role. According to Hunter (1994, p. 51), LDS teachings promote a relationship in which "the wife be... a companion equal and necessary in full partnership" and where decisions are made together, indicating a shared stewardship over family matters. Elder L. Tom Perry elaborates on this idea by saying, "A family does not have a president or vice president. According to Perry (2004) on page 71, "We have copresidents working 'together eternally for the good of their family'." Therefore, couples need to contribute to the family role equally if they want a happy family. So they could increase their relationship.

    Rejecting Hierarchical Traditions:

    Spouse need to rejecting traditions or social conventions that harm partners' equality like shaking hands when greeting someone that is opposite gender, standing up for someone that you drawn attention with, and making eye contact during a conversation. This is another key facts about the idea of stewardship in equal partnerships. In chapter 4 Elder Scott stated, "That is not the way of the Lord... That is a cruel, mistaken vision of marriage encouraged by Lucifer that every priesthood holder must reject,"(Scott, 2008, p. 46). Therefore, stewardship is about equality and respect for one another, not about power and dominance. This is also mentioned in (see Genesis 3:16), it says "I will surely multiply your pain in childbearing; in pain you shall bring forth children. Your desire shall be contrary to husband, but he shall rule over you."

    Encouraging One Another's Development:

    Encouraging one another's personal and spiritual development is an important part of stewardship in an equal relationship. This involves when couples supporting one another in accomplishing their personal goals. "You have always given me wings to fly, and I have loved you for it," (Hinckley, 2004, p. 85). President Gordon B. Hinckley said this an emotional reflection on his marriage and it was so meaningful. Therefore, this is another supporting point of stewardship mentioned by Elder Hinckley, which emphasize that each partner's have one heart, and mind in their decision making, family management, and in their responsability roles.

    Shared Domestic Responsibilities:

    The idea of stewardship is further developed in LDS teachings to include shared duties for household chores and parenting. As (Hudson & Miller, ch4, p.42) include Elder Packers quote into this chapter. "There is no task, however menial, connected with the care of babies, the nurturing of children, or with the maintenance of the home that is not [the husband's] equal obligation." This emphasizing the equal responsibility of both couples in these areas like their duties and responsability or parenting towards their childrens (Packer, 1989, p. 75). Therefore, a great example for shared domestic responsability are, firstly, sharing household chores shows a commitment of a spouse in relationship. When couples apply this in their relationship, it show mutual respest to each others time and contribution. Secondly, Enhancing communication and teamwork will help them to easily to manage their family decision and problem-solving. Last but not least, deepening emotional connection and appreciation. This will help couples feel satisfy and feel like both of them are value and important to one another.
