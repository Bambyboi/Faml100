Supporting Families across Generations

Relationships between Adult Children and their Parents

Researchers studying families have been looking at how adult children interact with their parents. This has made people question the idea that changes in society would lead to fewer interactions between the two generations. As people moved from the country to the cities in the early 1900s, experts predicted that family unity would weaken. They were afraid that adult children would grow apart from their parents when they had to move to towns for work and family reasons. This idea, which was summed up by Talcott Parsons' "isolated nuclear family" (1943), showed that ties between generations were not likely to get better in the future. Empirical studies, on the other hand, have shown that this myth is not true because many adult children still have close ties with their parents. Lye (1996) says that a large portion of adult children visit their parents regularly and maintain a strong emotional bond with them throughout their lives. Lawton, Silverstein, and Bengtson's (1994a) study shows that more than two-thirds of adult children talk to their parents at least once a week, if not every day. It seems likely that more than half of adult children live within an hour's drive of their parents based on this result. In addition, most people feel very close to their family members emotionally, which shows how strong these bonds are ("Lye, 1996").

Many things, like gender roles and big life events like death or divorce, can change the relationship between an adult child and their parents. Most of the time, these family ties are centered around women, especially young girls. Lye (1996) and Silverstein and Bengtson (1997) say that they often act as "kin keepers" and are able to get along better with their dads and moms. This difference between men and women shows how important it is for women to stay close to their families. It can be harder for these bonds to stay strong after a divorce, especially between dads and their grown children. This is really true when the dads aren't together. Both Booth and Amato (1994) and Silverstein and Bengtson (1997) say that when a parent gets separated, the child and father live farther away from each other and spend less time together. All of these things hurt the bond between the dad and the kid. When a parent separates from their kids, things don't have to get worse. Remember that a parent's divorce can affect these ties in both good and bad ways, depending on when it happened (while the kids were still at home or after they left). And this shows how difficult these changes are that make it hard for parents and grown children to talk to each other.

The Role of Grandparents in Extended Families

Grandparents play a bigger and bigger part in today's homes than they did in the past. As healthcare gets better and life expectancy rises, people live longer and happier lives. Grandparents can be more active in their grandchildren's lives for longer periods of time. In the same time period as this change, families became smaller in popular culture. This meant that the grandmother's part was more significant. Unlike in the past, when many families lived in one house, grandparents today don't have to deal with this issue very often. These differences happened because it was tough to tell the difference between being a parent and a grandparent in large families. After being parents, it's normal for them to become grandma. Now that they are free, they can spend more time and money on their grandkids. A study by Silverstein and Marenco (2001) shows that most grandparents in the US think their role is very important. The grandparents are more active. Numerous grandparents also spend a lot of time talking and getting close to their grandchildren.

In order to help their grandchildren grow as people, grandparents do many things. As grandparents, they look out for their grandkids, teach them, and give them someone to talk to. A lot of people, including President Boyd K. Packer, have talked about how important it is for kids to stay in touch with their grandparents. The grandchildren will live longer if they spend time with their grandparents. Having close grandparents can help grandchildren who are having behavior issues or signs of depression (Henderson, Hayslip, Sanders, & Louden, 2009; Ruiz & Silverstein, 2007). These kinds of interactions are very helpful, and has shown that grandchildren who are mentally close to their grandparents are less likely to have these symptoms. A study by Attar-Schwartz, Tan, Buchanan, Flouri, and Griggs (2009) also found that grandchildren who spend time with their grandparents are more likely to be respectful. This effect is very clear in homes with only one parent or African American parents. The author prove the important grandparents are to the foundation of big families. Besides that, they show how important grandparents are for helping kids grow up healthy and mature.

Extended Family Support: Help from the Grandparent Generation

When grandparents have grown children or grandchildren, they offer a unique mix of emotional support, advice, and physical help. One of the most important things grandparents do to help their large families is this. Fingerman, Miller, Birditt, and Zarit (2009) say that their involvement can range from helping out once in a while, like babysitting, to playing more important parts in socializing and giving advice. It is very important for grandparents to be there for and help their grandkids, as the authors say. These kinds of help between generations are growing more and more important in today's world, where life's problems often call for more family support. And Elder Neal A. Maxwell agrees, calling grandparents the "strategic reserve" of the family (1997, p. 146), and Hagestad calls Granny "the Family National Guard" (1985). Grandparents help their grandchildren when they are in trouble. They know how to change from being an unobtrusive presence to an active part in their families' lives. They offer real help during times of trouble like illness, unemployment, or divorce, showing that the family has a strong support through generations.

More and more grandparents are taking on a more permanent role as main caregivers or custodial grandparents. This shows how the dynamics of extended families are changing. This is in addition to giving help from time to time. Landry-Meyer, Gerard, and Guzell (2005) say that the rising number of grandparents who raise their grandchildren is a sign of a change in society. Hayslip and Kaminski (2005) say that about 2.4 million grandparents have taken on the duty of taking care of their grandchildren. Given that this role reversal often happens when parents are unable to care for their grandkids because of problems with drugs or alcohol or because they are sick, it shows how dedicated grandparents are to their duties as caregivers for their grandchildren. According to academics like Hayslip and Kaminski (2005), there is a chance of more stress and health problems. However, the organization of custodial care has a lot of big benefits, such as helping grandkids do better in school and with their friends. Grannies and grandmas are very important in large families because they keep things stable and, in many cases, offer a chance for the next generation to learn and grow. This difficult mix of giving up things and being happy shows how important grandparents are to extended families.

Extended Family Support: Help for the Elderly

The support roles that occurs in extended families, especially for the elderly, shows how important it is for people of different generations to help each other out. This is because the amount of care that goes back and forth between adult children and their elderly parents changes a lot over time. At first, this relationship was based on mutual support. But as parents get older and have health problems that make it harder for them to care for themselves, something changes. In their study from 2006, Van Gallen and Dykstra talk about how emotional and physical help is passed down from one generation to the next. When the parents' health starts to get worse, this care is put to the test. According to Pinquart and Sorensen (2003), longer times of illness and longer life expectancy make the need for elder care greater. This shows how important it is for families to take care of their elderly members. Elder Dallin H. Oaks (1991) and President Ezra Taft Benson (1989) are two LDS Church leaders who have said again that we should respect and care for our elderly parents as the Bible says. They have also talked about how important it is for older people who are less independent to have help from family, church, and society. The idea that the family is the best place to get help for older family members is supported by this care framework, which not only follows moral and ethical rules but also meets the actual needs of older family members.

Even though it shows how strong and devoted the family is, caring for others is usually done by adult children, especially girls. But there are some problems that come with this heavy duty. The results of studies by Pinquart and Sorensen (2003) and Wolff and Kasper (2006) show that a lot of the care that crippled older people get is given by their family members. About thirty hours a week are spent on this task by these family members, and some of them give even more. The responsibilities of caring for an elderly person, their physical and mental limitations, and the chance of behavioral disorders can all make the caregiver very stressed, depressed, and sick. Even though this job is rewarding, it causes a lot of stress, depression, and health problems for the caregiver. The caregiver's health is also affected by how the family works, especially how sisterly or brotherly love or dislike them. According to Suitor and Pillemer (1993) and Strawbridge and Wallhagen (1991), this can have a big effect on how stressed people are and how decisions about care are made. Because of this, Elder Oaks and President Benson pushed for siblings to work together and for the main caregiver's hard work to be recognized. This has to be done so that the caregiver and the person receiving care can share the good and bad parts of caring for an elderly person. This way, the caregiver will be supported and there will be fewer arguments that could hurt the person receiving care.
