Supporting Families across Generations

Relationships between Adult Children and their Parents

The dynamics between adult children and their parents have been a focal point of family research, challenging earlier predictions of weakened bonds due to societal shifts. Early 20th-century scholars, alarmed by the migration from rural to urban areas, anticipated a decline in family cohesion, fearing adult children would become estranged from their parents upon moving to cities for work and family life. This notion, encapsulated in Talcott Parsons' concept of the “isolated nuclear family” (1943), suggested a bleak future for intergenerational connections. However, empirical studies have debunked this myth, revealing that adult children often maintain robust relationships with their parents. Lye (1996) notes that a significant majority of adult children frequently visit their parents and share a close emotional bond. Supporting this, Lawton, Silverstein, & Bengtson (1994a) found that over half of adult children live within an hour's drive from their parents, with more than two-thirds engaging in weekly, if not daily, communications. Furthermore, a vast majority express a deep emotional closeness, highlighting the enduring strength of these familial ties ("Lye, 1996").

The texture of adult child–parent relationships is nuanced, often colored by gender dynamics and life events such as divorce. Women, particularly daughters, tend to be at the heart of these familial connections, commonly acting as the "kin keepers" and nurturing closer bonds, particularly with their mothers (Lye, 1996; Silverstein & Bengtson, 1997). This gendered pattern underscores the pivotal role of women in sustaining family relationships. Conversely, the divorce of parents can strain these connections, especially between adult children and fathers. While widowhood does not necessarily impair parent-child relationships, divorce leads to less frequent visits, diminished emotional closeness, and greater physical distance, primarily affecting the father-child bond (Booth & Amato, 1994; Silverstein & Bengtson, 1997). Notably, the negative impact of parental divorce on these relationships tends to vary depending on whether the divorce occurred while the children were still at home or after they had left, illustrating the complexity of factors influencing adult child–parent dynamics.

The Role of Grandparents in Extended Families

The role of grandparents within extended families has evolved significantly, becoming increasingly central in the contemporary familial landscape. Advances in healthcare and changes in life expectancy mean adults are living longer, healthier lives, allowing them to engage more actively and for extended periods in their grandchildren's lives. This shift has coincided with a societal trend towards smaller family sizes, which has, in turn, elevated the significance of the grandparental role. Unlike in the past, where large family sizes could blur the lines between parenting and grandparenting roles due to overlapping generations within the household, today's grandparents typically do not face this dilemma. They often enter grandparenthood having completed their direct parenting responsibilities, enabling them to dedicate more attention and resources to their grandchildren. This heightened involvement is evidenced by findings from Silverstein and Marenco (2001), who report that a majority of grandparents in the United States consider their role extremely important, with significant numbers maintaining regular contact and feeling a strong emotional bond with their grandchildren.

Grandparents play a multifaceted role in supporting the emotional and social development of their grandchildren, serving not just as caregivers but also as mentors and confidants. The importance of fostering relationships between grandchildren and their grandparents has been underscored by figures such as President Boyd K. Packer, who emphasized the value of connecting young people with their elders. The benefits of such relationships are profound, with research indicating that emotional closeness to grandparents can mitigate behavioral issues and symptoms of depression in grandchildren (Henderson, Hayslip, Sanders, & Louden, 2009; Ruiz & Silverstein, 2007). Furthermore, grandparent involvement is linked to the promotion of prosocial behaviors among grandchildren, a benefit that is particularly pronounced in single-parent and African American families (Attar-Schwartz, Tan, Buchanan, Flouri, & Griggs, 2009). These findings highlight the integral role grandparents play in nurturing the well-being and moral development of future generations, underscoring the significance of their contribution to the extended family structure.

Extended Family Support: Help from the Grandparent Generation

Grandparents play a crucial role in the fabric of extended family support, offering a unique blend of emotional backing, advice, and practical assistance to both their adult children and grandchildren. Their involvement ranges from casual support, such as babysitting, to significant emotional and advisory roles, as detailed by Fingerman, Miller, Birditt, & Zarit (2009), who highlight the profound impact of grandparents' emotional support and advice. This intergenerational assistance has become increasingly important in contemporary society, where the complexities of modern life often necessitate additional family support. Dubbed "the Family National Guard" by Hagestad (1985) and further endorsed by Elder Neal A. Maxwell as the "strategic reserve" of the family (1997, p. 146), grandparents step up in times of crisis. They adeptly transition from a non-intrusive presence to active participants in their families' lives, offering tangible help during emergencies such as health issues, unemployment, or divorce, thus embodying a resilient support system that underpins the family structure.

In addition to episodic support, a growing number of grandparents assume a more permanent role as primary caregivers or custodial grandparents, a testament to the shifting dynamics within extended families. The increasing trend of grandparents raising their grandchildren, as reported by Landry-Meyer, Gerard, & Guzell (2005), points to a societal shift where approximately 2.4 million grandparents have taken on the custodial care of their grandchildren (Hayslip & Kaminski, 2005). This role reversal often stems from challenging circumstances, such as parental inability due to legal issues, substance abuse, or incapacity, showcasing the depth of grandparents' commitment to their familial duties. Despite the potential for increased stress and health challenges highlighted by researchers like Hayslip & Kaminski (2005), the custodial arrangement offers significant benefits, including improved academic performance and social behavior in grandchildren. This intricate blend of sacrifice and fulfillment underscores the indispensable role of grandparents within extended families, providing a stabilizing force and, in many cases, a redemptive opportunity for intergenerational nurture and growth.

Extended Family Support: Help for the Elderly

The support system within extended families, especially for the elderly, underscores the vital role of intergenerational assistance, where the flow of care between adult children and their aging parents shifts significantly over time. Initially characterized by reciprocity, this dynamic evolves as parents age, confronting health issues that limit their self-sufficiency. Van Gallen & Dykstra (2006) highlight the mutual emotional and practical support exchanged between generations, which faces a critical test when parents' health deteriorates. The increasing life expectancy, coupled with extended periods of disability as noted by Pinquart & Sörensen (2003), amplifies the need for elder care, emphasizing the family's role in providing for their aged members. LDS Church leaders, including Elder Dallin H. Oaks (1991) and President Ezra Taft Benson (1989), have echoed the scriptural mandate to honor and care for elderly parents, stressing the significance of familial, church, and community support in aiding the less independent elderly. This framework of care not only adheres to moral and ethical standards but also addresses the practical necessities of aging family members, reinforcing the notion of family as a cornerstone of support for its elderly members.

The responsibility of caregiving, often assumed by adult children, especially daughters, is a testament to family resilience and commitment but comes with its challenges. Research by Pinquart & Sörensen (2003) and Wolff & Kasper (2006) indicates that a substantial portion of care for the disabled elderly is provided by family members, who on average dedicate around 30 hours a week to this task, with some providing even more. This caregiving role, while fulfilling, is associated with significant stress, depression, and health issues for the caregiver, exacerbated by the demands of care, the elderly's physical and cognitive impairments, and potential behavioral issues. The caregiver's well-being is also influenced by family dynamics, particularly the support or criticism from siblings, which can significantly affect stress levels and the decision-making process regarding care arrangements (Suitor & Pillemer, 1993; Strawbridge & Wallhagen, 1991). Thus, cooperation among siblings and recognition of the primary caregiver's efforts are crucial, as advocated by Elder Oaks and President Benson, to share the burdens and blessings of elder care, ensuring the caregiver's support and reducing the likelihood of conflicts that could impact the care recipient's well-being.
