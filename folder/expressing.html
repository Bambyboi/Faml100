  • Positive Relationship Formation Education for Disadvantaged Young Adults: Young adults from disadvantaged backgrounds, such as those from low-income families or those without supportive role models in marriage, experience extra obstacles in developing committed, long-lasting partnerships. Relationship and marital outcomes for these people can be greatly impacted by relationship education specifically designed for them. As to the findings of Cox and Shirer (2009), a program designed for low-income parents of small children yielded "significant effects for communication and problem-solving skills and greater positive co-parenting attitudes and behaviors." In the same direction, positive results were found at one site with high participation levels in the Building Strong Families (BSF) study, which concentrated on low-income, newlywed couples and found "a pattern of significant, positive couple relationship and father involvement effects for African American couples" (Wood et al., 2010). These results highlight the relationship education to improve the romantic and parental relationships of young adults from poor backgrounds, indicating a need for more targeted and easily accessible for relationship education.

    • Helping Engaged Couples Build a Stronger Foundation for Marriage

  • Participation in Formal Premarital Education: It is important to provide formal premarital education in order to improve communication and problem-solving skills in engage couples, which are the key for overcoming the necessary difficulties of married life. According to Fawcett, Hawkins, Blanchard, & Carroll (2010), "premarital education for engaged couples is moderately effective at increasing couples’ communication skills" highlights the importance of this kind of training. Studies show that "couples who invest in formal premarital education are less likely to divorce in the early years of marriage" (Nock et al., 2008; Stanley, Amato, Johnson, & Markman, 2006), this emphasizes the programs' is working and help to lower early divorce rates in engage couples. It has been reported that "10 to 15 percent of couples taking premarital education classes call off the wedding because they come to believe that the relationship will not succeed" (Stanley, 2001) provides an example of the influence of these programs, showing that they help engage couples in making better decisions about their interaction and future together.
  • Participation in Early Marital Enrichment Programs: Introducing the idea of relationship education into the early stages of a marriage through enrichment programs can improve the happiness in marriage. It has been shown that these programs "modestly improve communication skills and enhance relationship quality" (Hawkins, Blanchard, Baldwin, & Fawcett, 2008; Blanchard, Hawkins, Baldwin, & Fawcett, 2009). A study with an 11-year follow-up that revealed "a significantly lower divorce rate in the treatment group that received the enrichment program" (Hahlweg & Richter, 2010) it provides strong proof of a significant long-term benefit. As an example of this program's accessibility and potential influence on marriage stability, consider the PREP (Prevention and Relationship Enhancement Program) curriculum, which is commonly utilized in Oklahoma. It provides free classes that not only aim to improve couples' communication and problem-solving skills, but are also widely available in social environments specially to those who have disadvantaged backgroung.
